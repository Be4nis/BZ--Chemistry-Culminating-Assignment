Bonding & VSPER Theory

Learning About VSEPR Theory

Valence Shell Electron Theory was a topic I found myself struggling with. When I was initially exposed to this I felt very confused I kept wondering how this related to elements and bonding. I now know that a VESPR diagram isused to explain the 3D geometry of molecules, kind of like a 3D lewis dot diagram. I practiced these diagrams for a long time, I would first drawing a compound using the Lewis Dot Diagram and then drawing the same compound with the VSEPR diagram. Overtime I finally understood that the VSEPR model is an extremely useful tool we to predict the structure of molecules and polyatmoic ions (as long as the middle atom is a non-metal.) I have certainly not mastered VSEPR theory but I am proud of myself for knowing how VSEPR models work.

Intermolecular Forces/Van Der Waals Forces

Intermolecular forces weren't a hard concept for me to understand. I knew that every covalent bond has forces keeping it together. London Dispersion Forces were easy to understand, every molecule has this force and if the molecule has a higher polarizability the stronger the LDFs are. Hydrogen bonding was also easy to identify, if a hydrogen molecule is connected with nitrogen, oxygen or flourine then it is a hydrogen bond. Dipole-Dipole forces happen when two polar molecules interact and ion-dipole forces happen when an ion and a dipole (polar molecule) interact. To master these forces I just had to know that LDF's weakest, followed by dipole-dipole, hydrogen bonding and then ion-dipole which is the strongest. Overall I found intermolecular forces simple and easy to understand.
